# The Accounting Flow

As transactions happen, they move through several stages.

```
Business Transaction
        ↓
Journal
        ↓
Ledger
        ↓
Trial Balance
        ↓
Financial Statements
```

Each stage serves a different purpose.

---

## 1. Business Transaction

Everything begins with something happening in the business.

Examples include:

- The owner invests money.
    
- Equipment is purchased.
    
- Inventory is bought.
    
- A customer buys products.
    
- Rent is paid.
    

Each of these events changes the company's financial position.

---

## 2. Journal

The **Journal** is the first place where transactions are recorded.

Think of it as the company's financial diary.

Entries are written **in chronological order**, showing:

- what happened,
    
- which accounts changed,
    
- and the debit and credit amounts.
    

Example:

|Debit|Credit|
|---|---|
|Cash £100|Share Capital £100|

This records the owner investing £100 into the company.

---

## 3. Ledger

The Journal tells us **what happened today**.

The **Ledger** tells us **everything that has ever happened to a particular account**.

Instead of being organised by date, it is organised by account.

For example:

### Cash

|Date|Debit|Credit|Balance|
|---|---|---|--:|
|Owner investment|£100||£100|
|Bought building||£60|£40|

### Buildings

|Date|Debit|Credit|Balance|
|---|---|---|--:|
|Purchased building|£60||£60|

Each account has its own running history.

---

## 4. Trial Balance

After transactions have been posted to the ledger, all account balances are collected into a **Trial Balance**.

Its purpose is to check that the bookkeeping is internally consistent.

For example:

|Account|Debit|Credit|
|---|--:|--:|
|Cash|£40||
|Buildings|£60||
|Share Capital||£100|

Notice that:

```
Total Debits  = £100
Total Credits = £100
```

If these totals are not equal, a bookkeeping error has occurred somewhere.

---

# Example: Starting a Company

Suppose shareholders invest **£100** to create a new company.

The company receives cash, so:

```
Debit   Cash             £100
Credit  Share Capital    £100
```

The company's assets increase, while the shareholders' ownership (equity) also increases.

---

# Buying a Building

Next, the company buys a building for **£60 cash**.

```
Debit   Buildings   £60
Credit  Cash        £60
```

The company still owns **£100** of assets.

Only their form has changed.

Before:

```
Cash        £100
```

After:

```
Cash         £40
Building     £60
```

---

# Buying Inventory

The company then buys inventory costing **£50**.

```
Debit   Inventory   £50
Credit  Cash        £50
```

Again, one asset decreases while another increases.

---

# Selling Inventory

Eventually, the company sells some inventory to a customer.

This actually creates **two separate transactions**.

## Step 1 — Record the sale

Suppose goods are sold for **£70**, and the customer will pay next month.

```
Debit   Accounts Receivable    £70
Credit  Sales Revenue          £70
```

The company has earned revenue, even though it hasn't received the cash yet.

Instead, it now has an asset called **Accounts Receivable**, representing money owed by the customer.

---

## Step 2 — Record the cost of the goods sold

The inventory that was sold originally cost the company **£50**.

That inventory no longer belongs to the company, so it must be removed from the books.

```
Debit   Cost of Sales    £50
Credit  Inventory        £50
```

This follows the **matching principle**, which says that the cost of generating revenue should be recognised in the same period as the revenue itself.

The sale therefore produces:

- **Revenue:** £70
    
- **Expense:** £50
    
- **Profit:** £20
    

---

# From Transactions to Financial Statements

Throughout the year:

- every transaction is recorded in the **Journal**,
    
- posted to the **Ledger**,
    
- checked in the **Trial Balance**,
    
- and finally summarised into the **Balance Sheet** and **Income Statement**.
    

At the end of the accounting period, the company's profit is transferred into **Retained Earnings**, an equity account.

This is what connects the two financial statements:

```
Income Statement
        ↓
Net Profit
        ↓
Retained Earnings
        ↓
Balance Sheet
```

The Income Statement explains **how the company made or lost money**, while the Balance Sheet shows **where that profit ended up** as part of the owners' equity.
